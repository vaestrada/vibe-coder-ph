# Filipino Tech/Event Blogger Style Guide

A reference for writing blog posts that sound like a real Filipino tech/event/community blogger, not an AI.

---

## Bloggers Studied

### 1. Carlo Ople — Unbox PH (Founder)
**Style:** Personal, emotionally honest, conversational first-person. Opens with a scenario or feeling, not a thesis statement. Uses analogy freely ("It's as if I saw a delectable piece of Salmon sashimi but had to spit it out after eating it because it was spoiled"). Transitions by narrating a shift in his own emotions or observations ("Then something started to happen"). Closes with a call to action that feels like a pep talk from a friend, not a corporate CTA. Quotes mentors and teachers naturally.

- **Opening style:** Drops you into a moment. "As of this writing I have around 16 gadgets... still in their boxes here at home." No throat-clearing.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Mix of long, winding sentences and punchy short ones. "It's understandable though since we are a nation that's hurting. Hurting badly."
- **Signature move:** The one-liner paragraph for emphasis. "Peace!"

### 2. D.J. Manjares — Medium (PH Tech Critic)
**Style:** Opinionated, unfiltered, funny. Writes like he's ranting to a friend at a coffee shop. Uses pop culture references, Filipino cultural context, and doesn't hold back. Highly conversational with parentheticals and asides.

- **Opening style:** Bold declarative hook. "Ah yes, the Filipino tech scene: filled with glitz, glamour, cool gadgets to salivate over, and infighting—lots of infighting."
- **Transitions:** Conversational pivots. "And then there's the tech websites." "Now let's get down starting with..."
- **Signature moves:** Direct address to the reader. Ratings with star emojis. Parenthetical commentary like "(famously known as Pinoy Techdad, who is I believe, one of the last remaining honest Filipino tech reviewers right there, and he suffered the effects of getting booted out of the OEM Glazers Mafia)". Long self-aware disclaimers.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Long flowing rant sentences punctuated by blunt one-liners. "If you're looking for the freshest news, PTG sucked."

### 3. Mike Martinez — #StartupPH Chronicles (Community Builder / Mentor)
**Style:** First-person storytelling mixed with cultural critique. Opens with personal context before pivoting to analysis. Uses Filipino cultural concepts (hiya, pakikisama, "happy-go-lucky") to explain startup behavior. Direct and sometimes blunt.

- **Opening style:** Personal timeline anchor. "It has been 4 years since I came back here and now situated in Cebu."
- **Transitions:** Numbered challenges or points, but not in a listicle way. More like a mentor walking you through lessons. "So here we go..."
- **Signature moves:** The mentor anecdote. "A perfect example, would be last week when I spoke at Ateneo University..." Uses real stories with real outcomes. Drops hard truths: "A simple landing page seems to be enough to buy-in a founder status and everyone is buying in like this is a poker tournament."
- **Cultural commentary:** Naturally weaves in Filipino traits as both strengths and challenges. Quotes General Luna. References startup weekends, incubators, real institutional names.

### 4. Vince Golangco — WhenInManila / vincegolangco.com
**Style:** Motivational, direct, slightly self-help flavored. Short punchy paragraphs. Heavy use of quotes and references. Very first-person, lifestyle-forward.

- **Opening style:** Quote or bold statement. "Procrastination is like masturbation, 'cause in the end, you're only fucking yourself."
- **Transitions:** Short bridging sentences. "Now, be like the Greek Goddess Nike and 'Just Do It!'"
- **Signature moves:** The call-to-action ending. "NOW is the time. It's all you have!" Mixing Western references with Filipino sensibility.
- **Context-setting:** "When In Manila, good personal hygiene is one of the most important aspects..."

### 5. Marlo Yonocruz — marloyonocruz.com (Filipino-American Creator/Blogger)
**Style:** Warm, structured but casual. Uses emojis naturally (👋, 🥳, 💪🏻). Self-referential humor. Long-form personal reflection that reads like a letter to friends.

- **Opening style:** Friendly greeting. "Hello friends, followers, and various people on the internet 👋 Happy New Year's Eve! 🎊"
- **Transitions:** Parenthetical asides and casual connectors. "(1 day better than last year haha)"
- **Signature moves:** The BAG framework (Blessings, Achievements, Goals). Calls things "super fun and a game-changer." Uses "haha" naturally in prose.
- **Long-form engagement:** Links to past year reviews, creating a serialized relationship with the reader.

## Common Filipino English Writing Patterns

### Sentence Structure
- **Front-loaded reactions before analysis.** Filipino writers react FIRST, explain AFTER. "Grabe, ang dami na nilang ginawa." Not "They accomplished a lot, which was impressive."
- **Shorter paragraphs.** 2-4 sentences max. Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis are common and feel natural.
- **Parenthetical asides everywhere.** "(and honestly, I didn't expect it)" or "(which is a whole other story)"
- **Run-on enthusiasm.** When excited, sentences get longer and pile on details. When making a serious point, they cut to one sharp line.
- **Questions as transitions.** "So what does that mean for us?" "But here's the thing — why?"

### Tone & Voice
- **Conversational authority.** Not academic, not corporate. Like someone who knows their stuff explaining it to a friend. "Let me break it down."
- **Self-deprecating humor.** "I messed up. Like, badly."
- **Direct address.** "You know that feeling when..." or "If you've been in the scene long enough..."
- **Emotional honesty.** Filipino writers don't hedge feelings. "It was heartbreaking." "I was so proud." Not "It was somewhat disappointing."
- **Humble brags done casually.** "I've been doing this since 2016 (yeah, it's been a while)"

### Openings That Work (Filipino Style)
1. **The scene-drop.** Start in the middle of a moment. "Last Saturday, I was standing in front of 200 people and I had no idea what I was going to say."
2. **The personal anchor.** "Two years ago, I moved back to Manila."
3. **The bold claim.** "The Filipino tech scene has a problem nobody wants to talk about."
4. **The reaction.** "I didn't expect to cry at a tech event. But here we are."
5. **The question.** "Have you ever tried to explain to your tita what you do for a living?"

### Transitions (NOT Cringy Subheadings)
Filipino bloggers transition with:
- **Narrative shifts:** "Then something started to happen." "And that's when it clicked."
- **Conversational pivots:** "But here's the thing." "Now let's talk about..." "So here we go..."
- **Time jumps:** "Fast forward to last month." "Two hours into the event..."
- **Emotional beats:** "It was hard. But it was worth it."
- **Contrast:** "On one hand... but on the other..."
- **Direct address:** "Now, I know what you're thinking."

### Closings
- **The call to action as a friend:** "Let's do our share." "Go build something."
- **The reflection:** "Looking back, I wouldn't change a thing."
- **The one-word/phrase sign-off:** "Peace!" "Onward." "Let's go 🚀"
- **The forward look:** "I can't wait to see what next year brings."

## Taglish Flavor (Natural Code-Switching)

### When to Drop Filipino
The key is that Filipino words/phrases appear at **emotional peaks** or when English feels too formal for the sentiment. They should feel involuntary, like the writer couldn't help it.

### Natural Taglish Expressions for Blog Writing

**Reactions / Interjections:**
- "Grabe." — Intense. Used when something is overwhelming (good or bad).
- "Ang galing." — "That's amazing." Pure admiration.
- "Sobrang [adjective]." — "Super [adjective]." Intensifier. "Sobrang solid ng lineup."
- "Legit." — Used in both English and Taglish. "Legit na legit 'to." or just "Legit."
- "Solid." — High praise. "Solid yung event."
- "Astig." — Cool/impressive in a tough way.
- "Lodi." — Idol (reversed, Filipino slang). "Lodi talaga."

**Discourse Markers (the glue of Taglish):**
- "naman" — softener/emphasis. "Okay naman." "Game changer naman talaga."
- "talaga" — "really/truly." "Ang galing talaga." "Mahirap talaga."
- "kasi" — "because." "Kasi hindi naman ganun kadali."
- "'di ba?" — "right?" Tag question seeking agreement.
- "parang" — "like/as if." "Parang ang dami mong nalalaman."
- "pala" — surprise/realization. "Grabe pala 'to."
- "na" — "already/now." "Okay na." "Let's go na."

**Phrases That Feel Natural in English-First Blog Writing:**
- "Game changer talaga." (authentically better than "It was truly a game changer.")
- "Ang daming tao!" (when describing crowd size at events)
- "Buti na lang..." ("Good thing that..." / "Thankfully...")
- "Iba talaga 'pag..." ("It hits different when...")
- "Sige na nga." ("Fine, okay." Reluctant agreement.)
- "Bahala na." ("Come what may." / "YOLO" Filipino edition.)
- "Puro [noun] lang." ("All just [noun]." Dismissive.)
- "Wala pa rin tatalo sa..." ("Nothing still beats...")

**Taglish for Community/Event Writing:**
- "Daming dumating!" — "So many people came!"
- "Ang init pero worth it naman." — "It was hot but worth it."
- "Sana all." — "I wish everyone could have this." (Aspirational/envious)
- "Kilig moment." — A moment of excitement/giddiness.
- "Tamang-tama." — "Just right / perfect timing."

### Rules for Natural Taglish
1. **Don't translate after using it.** If you write "Grabe," don't follow with "(which means intense)." Trust the reader or let context do the work.
2. **Keep it to 5-10% of the text.** You're writing in English with Filipino flavor, not the other way around.
3. **Use it at emotional moments,** not for technical content. The AI model specs stay in English. The "grabe" comes when you describe the crowd reaction.
4. **Filipino words cluster around feelings, food, weather, and people.** Technical terms stay English.
5. **Don't force it.** If a Filipino expression doesn't come naturally in a sentence, leave it out. Forced Taglish reads worse than no Taglish at all.

## Anti-Patterns (What Makes Writing Sound Like AI)

| AI Pattern | Filipino Blogger Pattern |
|---|---|
| "In this blog post, we will explore..." | Just start. Drop the reader into a scene or reaction. |
| "It's important to note that..." | "Here's the thing." or just state it directly. |
| "Let's dive in!" | "So here we go..." or "Tara, let's get into it." |
| "Without further ado..." | Never. Just transition naturally. |
| "In conclusion..." | End when you're done. Maybe a "Peace!" or a forward look. |
| Perfect parallel structure in every list | Mix list items unevenly. Real people don't write symmetrical lists. |
| Em dashes everywhere | Filipinos use commas, periods, and parentheses. Occasional em dash at most. |
| "Firstly... Secondly... Thirdly..." | "First off... And then... Oh, and..." |
| "This is a game-changer." (alone) | "Game changer talaga 'to." or "This changed everything for us." |
| Consistent tone throughout | Real writers get more excited, more serious, more casual depending on the section. |
| "I had the privilege of..." | "I got to..." or "We managed to..." |
| "The event was a resounding success." | "The event was packed. Like, standing room only." |

## Quick Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Does the opening drop into a moment, reaction, or bold claim (not a preamble)?
- [ ] Are there single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis?
- [ ] Do transitions feel like a person talking, not section headers?
- [ ] Is there at least one real anecdote or specific detail?
- [ ] Are Filipino expressions used at emotional peaks (not scattered randomly)?
- [ ] Does the tone shift naturally (excited → reflective → forward-looking)?
- [ ] Would you actually say this out loud to a friend?
- [ ] Is the closing short and genuine, not a summary of everything you just said?
- [ ] Are em dashes limited to 1-2 max across the entire piece?
