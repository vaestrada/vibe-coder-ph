- **Early January 2026**, right after New Year's — Jayson mentioned he'd be coming home to the Philippines

## Deep Editorial Q&A (for English Blog CTA Section)

### Q1: The moment you almost quit / what planning actually cost

**The loneliness of the vision:**
- Unlike PSYSC or EMC² events where a whole organization works together — this was essentially a **one-man operation** at the start
- He was the ONLY person with both the vision and the experience to execute it
- No committee structure, no org behind him — just himself, AI tools, and the goal

**"I can vibe code this event":**
- Made it a personal goal to leverage AI for *everything* in the pre-event phase:
  - **Sponsor/participant outreach** — AI-drafted emails, personalized follow-ups via ChatGPT/Gemini
  - **Legal documents** — memorandum agreements, partnership docs
  - **Creative assets** — mascots via image generation, social media captions via ChatGPT/Gemini
  - **Website** — event page, registration forms, check-in system, all vibe coded
  - **Database** — Supabase backend, also vibe coded
  - **Newsletters** — countdown reminders, what-to-bring/expect emails sent to all registrants
  - **Certificates** — personalized generation + mass email via Resend
  - **Lead generation** — used Bright Data MCP to extract emails from school orgs and tech communities on Facebook
  - **Automated outreach** — personalized emails to potential org partners
- "That makes a one-man person work like a team of 10" — one person covering creative committee, marketing committee, promotion committee, guest speaker coordination, all powered by AI

**The venue escalation moment:**
- Got the Institute of Civil Engineering theater — **256-seat capacity**
- Originally planned for 15 people at a bar → now needed 200-300+ to fill a university theater
- This was the anxiety inflection point: "Oh no, we're going to create a big event now"
- Had to dramatically scale strategy: Facebook/Instagram ads, boosted posts, LinkedIn promotion, reaching out to school orgs and tech communities as amplification partners
- Partner org strategy: if he could gauge how many orgs partnered, he could estimate expected attendance

**The physical cost:**
- **3-4 hours of sleep** during planning period
- **Skipped meals** regularly
- All while holding a **full-time day job as an AI engineer**
- "Double time, triple time" — the constant hustle to promote, gain traction, boost engagement, grow followers

### Q2: His own origin gap — what Viron didn't know at UP

**Course: Applied Physics** — UP Diliman
- Very technical, very mathematical
- "I didn't know anything about the real world, about how to work"
- Constant self-doubt: *"Will I ever use this knowledge? These equations? Will I land a job from this kind of course?"*

**The education gap that shaped everything:**
- College was always about **research, study, read**
- **Never** about **create, build, ship**
- Never taught to build products — tangible or digital
- Never taught to build a portfolio or cultivate a professional skill set
- The *mindset* was never about creation — always purely academic/theoretical
- This is the exact gap Gen AI to Z was designed to address: showing students that building, creating, and shipping is possible — and AI makes it accessible even if your education never taught you how

**Q2 expanded: No one showed him the way — he carved his own path**
- Nobody gave him a career roadmap or showed him "a different way"
- Had support from **EMC² Fraternity brothers** — advice, connections, contacts, moral support ("everything will be alright, you'll land a job eventually, just do this, do that")
- Girlfriend supported him through Gen AI to Z planning specifically
- But the direction? Found it completely alone.

**The full career journey (the case study / synthesis):**
1. **Applied Physics, UP Diliman** — specialization in **photonics**. Didn't want to work in a dark lab doing experiments whose results wouldn't be visible in the near future. Couldn't see the real-world impact.
2. **Post-graduation struggle** — graduated with no work lined up. Spent a long time looking for jobs. Did sideline tutoring to get by.
3. **Masters in Marine Science** — completely different field. Wanted to do science but with field work, in the ocean. Philippines is surrounded by water — practical, tangible, meaningful. Gets asked all the time: "Why marine science when your bachelor's is applied physics?"
4. **The AI bridge** — His thesis: **classification of marine habitats using satellite images**. Classifying satellite imagery pixel by pixel — coral, seagrass, seaweed, rubble, rock, deep water. Used GoPro cameras submerged in water for geolocated ground truth data, extracted georeferenced video frames into images. This was his foundation in **computer vision, machine learning, and remote sensing**.
5. **Into corporate AI** — transitioned into AI/ML work professionally. Became an **AI engineer, AI solutions architect**. Has not yet finished the master's thesis (only thesis left).
6. **Leveraged ChatGPT early** — back in 2023-2024 when ChatGPT was still in its "infancy stages," he was already using it to **land jobs, write emails, do work**. Got the skill set and experience early.
7. **The creative evolution** — wanted to prove that AI **doesn't dumb us down, it makes us more creative**. Pushed himself to be more creative: musically, visually, in event production. This led directly to Gen AI to Z.
8. **The meta-thesis** — "No one has really thought about it yet: **organizing an AI event using AI.**" The event itself is the proof of concept for everything the event teaches.

**"There's no clear path"** — Applied Physics → Marine Science → AI engineer → organizing an AI summit. The journey makes no linear sense on paper, but every step built the foundation for the next. This IS the message for young Filipinos: your path doesn't have to be a straight line.

### Q7: The uncomfortable truth — Silicon Valley, the Philippines, and AI

**The current job landscape (the honest picture):**
- Recent graduates and even undergrads/senior high — regardless of course (IT, Economics, Nursing, top 5 courses) — end up in **BPO/call center** work
- It's the easiest entry point: high demand/supply, Filipino English proficiency, decent-ish pay (better than waiter, delivery rider, other part-time options)
- Even **skilled professionals** (developers, data analysts, business analysts, data scientists) work **night shifts** serving US/Western clients and corporations
- Virtual assistants, remote workers — sacrificing sleep for companies outside the Philippines
- "The professionals, the skilled professionals are also working on a night shift. And we are working for corporations or companies outside of the Philippines, which is a sad truth."

**The deeper problem — employee mindset:**
- "We're on an employee mindset" — Filipinos are skilled and talented but lack the right resources, support, and exposure
- This has been the pattern for a **very long time** — not new, deeply systemic
- The default path: graduate → get hired → serve foreign clients → repeat. No creation, no building, no ownership.

**AI as game changer — closing the gap:**
- AI closes the **technical gap** — even with limited resources, limited opportunities, limited support
- With AI leverage, Filipinos can transition: **employees → builders, creators, CEOs, leaders, innovators**
- Not just serving foreign clients as VAs/remote devs → **building startups, promoting industries, boosting the Philippine economy**
- "We can uplift the Philippine society"

**The Philippines as invisible AI workforce (needs web research to back up):**
- Filipinos are actually **AI trainers** — outsourced via companies like TaskUs, RemoteTask to build training data for the large language models the world uses
- Filipinos doing repetitive annotation/labeling tasks that build the foundation of AI models
- Meanwhile, Filipino developers, AI engineers, software devs work remotely for US/Western companies on night shifts
- "The Philippines is actually the workforce of the AI economy" — but this isn't highlighted or documented by mainstream media
- **Key insight for the blog:** The people who TRAIN the AI models are the same people who should be LEVERAGING those models to build their own futures. The irony is powerful.

**The call to action by career path:**
- **If you love your course** → leverage AI to land a better job, to level up in your field
- **Civil engineering, electrical engineering, marketing, creative** → AI helps in every domain
- **CS/CE/IT students who want to build** → vibe coding lets you ship apps/products quickly without mastering every technical language
- **Frustrated call center agents, blue collar workers** → learn AI, learn vibe coding in your free time, build a portfolio, land better jobs
- **The promise:** "This is a gateway and I'm sharing it for free. We will be sharing more for free, building more events, organizing more for free — to open this knowledge, this idea, this mindset."

**The raw emotional core:**
- "Not stuck in this daily routine of suffering — working just to get an ample amount to work the following day, to live another day and work another day. That's so capitalist."
- "But with the help of AI, maybe we can build better lives, a better Philippine society."

**On "Silicon Valley of the Philippines":**
- It's aspirational but rooted in real potential
- "We don't have that yet. But we have a lot of talent. We are the workforce of the world."
- The ingredients exist (talent, English proficiency, AI training experience, developer skill base) — what's missing is the **mindset shift from employee to builder** and the **support ecosystem**
- That's what Vibe Coders PH and Gen AI to Z are trying to build

### Q8: What you're actually afraid of

**The core fear: an empty venue.**
- Secured the venue (ICE theater, 256 capacity, expandable to 300-350 with extra chairs and standing room) in **early February**
- The pressure to fill it was constant — "it's going to be embarrassing if I only have few participants. Embarrassing to my guest speakers, to the organizers, to the sponsors."
- This was the dominant anxiety throughout the entire planning period

**How he overcame it — the org partner discovery (AI-powered):**
- Before the event, Viron **knew nothing about IT school organizations** — didn't know GDG, AWS Cloud Clubs, DEVCON Manila, JPCS, etc. existed
- Using **vibe coding tools and Bright Data MCP** for web scraping, he discovered an entire ecosystem of tech communities and school orgs he never knew about
- "I found out: hey, I should tap IT school organizations and tech communities. I should reach out to schools outside UP — TIP, FEU, UST, NU, PUP, and others I can't even remember or pronounce."
- Without AI, he could only think of reaching out to UP orgs — which may or may not have been interested
- AI-assisted scraping and outreach **expanded his universe of possible partners** dramatically

**The partnership ask (deliberately simple):**
- Requirements for org partners: **(1)** at least 10 members register and attend, **(2)** amplify social media posts — share, engage, comment
- "I want it to be community-driven"
